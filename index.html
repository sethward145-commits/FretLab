Tritone Substitution — the jazz reharmonization trick

A tritone substitution replaces a dominant 7th chord with another dominant 7th chord whose root is a tritone (6 semitones) away. It works because the two chords share the same guide tones — the 3rd and 7th — just swapped.

Why it works: In a ii–V–I in A, the V chord is E7. Its 3rd (G#) and 7th (D) are the two most important tones for resolving to the I chord. The tritone sub B♭7 has those same two notes as its 7th and 3rd — so it resolves just as smoothly, but with chromatic bass motion (B♭ → A) instead of a fifth drop.

How to use it: Anywhere you see a V7 chord, try the dominant 7th a tritone away. Common examples: replace G7→C with D♭7→C, or replace E7→A with B♭7→A. Works in blues, jazz, gospel, and neo-soul.
